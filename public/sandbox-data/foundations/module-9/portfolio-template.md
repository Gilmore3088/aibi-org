# AiBI-P Portfolio Work Product Template

Use this template for each of the four items in your capstone portfolio. Each item should demonstrate a real AI skill you built during the course, applied to your actual role at a community bank or credit union. The strongest portfolios show measurable impact, honest limitations, and professional judgment.

---

## 1. The Skill

Describe the AI skill you built: what it does, which platform you used, and the RTFC prompt you developed. Include enough context that a reviewer unfamiliar with your institution can understand the business problem you solved.

A strong submission names the specific department, process, and frequency (e.g., "weekly BSA exception triage in our operations department"). Vague descriptions like "I used AI to help with compliance" do not demonstrate practitioner-level competence.

---

## 2. Sample Input

Provide the real or realistic data you fed to the AI. If you used actual institutional data, anonymize it completely: replace names, account numbers, and identifying details with plausible substitutes.

Strong submissions include enough data to show the complexity of the task. A single sentence of input rarely demonstrates a meaningful skill. Show the kind of messy, real-world input the AI must handle, including edge cases or ambiguous entries where possible.

---

## 3. Raw Output

Paste the unedited output the AI produced. Do not clean it up. The purpose of this section is to show what the model actually generated before you applied professional judgment.

Strong submissions preserve formatting, any errors, and any hallucinated or questionable content. Reviewers need to see the gap between raw AI output and your final product. Omitting mistakes undermines the credibility of your annotations in the next section.

---

## 4. Edited Output + Annotation

Present your edited, production-ready version of the output. Then annotate every material change you made: what you changed, why you changed it, and what professional knowledge informed your decision.

Strong submissions show substantive edits, not just grammar fixes. Flag factual corrections, regulatory nuances the model missed, tone adjustments for your audience, and any content you removed as unreliable. The annotations are where you demonstrate that you are the expert, not the model.
