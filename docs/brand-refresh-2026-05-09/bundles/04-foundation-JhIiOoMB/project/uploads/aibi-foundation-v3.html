02 · The A-B-C Framework

Three pillars.
One necessary sequence.

The sequence is not optional. Institutions that skip A and B and go straight to C have AI initiatives that fail at adoption. The framework exists because the fears have to be cleared in order.

A
Pillar One
Accessible
Before staff will use AI safely or efficiently, they have to believe they can use it at all. This pillar exists to overcome fear — through personal, low-stakes, immediately rewarding experiences with tools anyone already has access to.
Tools introduced: ChatGPT (OpenAI), Claude (Anthropic), Gemini (Google), Microsoft Copilot (M365 — already in most banks)
Optional builders: Lovable, Bolt.new, Replit — for those who want to build something, not just use something
The opening experience: Personal first. Build something for yourself — a recipe organizer, a trip itinerary, a personal budget template — before anything banking-related
The business pivot: "Now what are your top 3–5 problems at work? What can you solve with what you just learned?"
Not required in sequence: This progression can span sessions, days, or weeks. It is a phase, not a block of consecutive hours
Assessment first: Every learner completes the readiness assessment before being assigned curriculum. Accessibility is the default starting point for low-score responders
B
Pillar Two
Boundary-Safe
Every recommendation assumes a regulated institution. The security pillar addresses the first objection anyone says out loud — "Is this safe for our members? For our examiners?" — with specifics, not reassurances.
Primary source documents: US Treasury "AI in Financial Services" report · GAO-25-107197 (May 2025) · FSOC Annual Report · Interagency TPRM Guidance (2023)
Regulatory frameworks that apply to AI: SR 11-7 (Model Risk Management, applied to AI/ML models) · Interagency TPRM Guidance (AI vendors as third-party relationships) · ECOA/Reg B (for AI in credit decisions) · BSA/AML (for AI in transaction monitoring)
What never goes in a public LLM: Member PII (account numbers, SSNs, addresses) · MNPI (for institutions with publicly traded holding companies) · Proprietary credit policy detail · Specific examination findings
The enterprise vs. public distinction: Public ChatGPT ≠ ChatGPT Enterprise (data not used for training). Microsoft Copilot within M365 has contractual data protections. This distinction is taught explicitly, not assumed
What examiners currently look for: Model documentation · AI use case inventory · Third-party due diligence records · Explainability for credit-adjacent AI · Governance structure ownership
Shadow AI: Staff are already using AI on personal devices without guidelines. This is addressed directly — the choice is not AI vs. no AI, it is governed vs. ungoverned AI
C
Pillar Three
Capable
Once people are using AI safely and confidently, we empower every banker to become a builder and problem-solver. You never know who has the best idea in the room — a teller who's been frustrated by a manual process for three years might build the automation that saves the operations team 10 hours a week.
The shift: From "AI saves time" to "AI gives every banker the tools to solve their own problems." The efficiency ratio impact follows — but the frame that converts staff is empowerment, not productivity
What this looks like in practice: Loan officer builds their own pipeline dashboard. BSA officer builds their own regulatory change tracker. Teller builds an automated exception report. IT was not involved in any of these
The tools: Power Automate (already in M365), Copilot Studio, Zapier, Make — for automations. ChatGPT/Claude for content and analysis. Lovable/Bolt.new for staff who want to build actual apps
The efficiency ratio impact: Documented in NIE reduction terms for leadership. Community bank FDIC data median: ~65% efficiency ratio. Top-quartile technology-forward institutions: 55–60%. The gap is real; the specific claim requires honest sourcing (see Section 07)
The builder frame for CEOs: "What if every department could solve its own biggest problem without waiting for IT? That's what the Capable pillar builds."
03 · Curriculum Architecture

Assessment-first.
Then personal. Then professional.

The curriculum does not begin with modules. It begins with an assessment that determines where the learner starts and what path they follow. The opening experience is personal and fun — fear removed before business application begins.

Core Philosophy
People earn the designation when their work product meets the standard — not when they complete the hours. "If you submitted this output to your supervisor without saying AI was involved, would anyone know — and would anyone push back on the substance?" That is the BAI quality standard.

The BAI Brief — Monthly Email Template

Format & Structure
Subject formula: One specific data point stated as a fact. Example: "Loan doc QC is costing your operations team 4 hours a week. Here's the workflow."

Three sections, ~400 words total:
1. The Metric (2–3 sentences) — one data point from FDIC quarterly banking profiles, Jack Henry, Cornerstone Advisors, Gartner, or the AIEOG Lexicon. Always cited with source. Always community-bank specific. Never use a statistic that cannot be traced to a named publication.
2. The Use Case (150–200 words) — one specific automation: the problem (hours/week), the tool (specific), setup time, ROI calculation.
3. The Regulatory Note (75–100 words) — one development from CFPB, OCC, FDIC, NCUA, or FinCEN. Links to primary source. Flags where legal review is required.

Close: One sentence. Either "Forward this to your CFO" or a link to the assessment or ROI calculator. Never more than one ask.
07 · Data & Sources — Research Library

Every number sourced.
Every claim defensible.

A CFO will ask where the numbers come from. A compliance officer will verify the regulatory claims. Every data point used in AiBI materials is classified below: sourced, calculable, or directional. Never use unsourced data in a client-facing document.

Retire Immediately — Two Claims That Cannot Be Defended
(1) "AI-enabled peers run at 58.1% efficiency ratio." No published benchmark exists for this specific claim. Replace with FDIC-sourced language: "Community banks run a median ~65% efficiency ratio; the industry-wide figure including large banks is ~55.7% (FDIC Q4 2024)."

(2) "FFIEC-aware training." The FFIEC has not issued AI-specific training guidance. No such standard exists. Replace with the specific regulatory frameworks listed in Section 07c below.
