## WHAT THIS DOCUMENT IS

This is the comprehensive PRD for AiBI-L — the leader workshop of the AiBI curriculum. It specifies every session, every activity, every deliverable, every facilitation note, and every assessment requirement.

AiBI-L is fundamentally different from AiBI-Foundation and AiBI-S. It is not an online course. It is not self-paced. It is not a cohort that meets weekly. It is a single-day, in-person workshop designed for the people who sign the checks, set the strategy, and answer to examiners and boards of directors. Everything about the design — the pacing, the deliverables, the facilitation style — reflects that these participants are senior leaders with limited time, high expectations, and zero tolerance for anything that feels like a training class.

### Why AiBI-L Exists

AiBI-Foundation creates personal AI skills. AiBI-S creates departmental AI champions. But neither of those programs produces the institutional decision that unlocks budget, governance, and strategic commitment. That decision happens in the C-suite and the boardroom. AiBI-L exists to equip the executives who make that decision with the strategic framework, the regulatory confidence, the financial modeling, and the board-ready materials to make it well.

The business outcome of AiBI-L is not the credential — it is the board presentation that the executive delivers after the workshop. That presentation, built with their institution's actual numbers and regulatory context, is the vehicle for institutional AI transformation.

### The Institutional Relationship Prerequisite

AiBI-L does not require AiBI-Foundation or AiBI-S certification as a prerequisite. It requires an institutional relationship — meaning the workshop is booked through a sales conversation, not a self-serve checkout. This is deliberate:

1. **Pre-workshop data gathering.** The facilitator needs the institution's actual financial data (efficiency ratio, FTE count, department structure, technology stack) to customize Sessions 3 and 4. This data is collected during a pre-workshop planning call, not during the workshop itself.
2. **Participant alignment.** A workshop with a CEO, a CFO, a COO, and two board members produces a fundamentally different outcome than a workshop with a single VP. The sales conversation ensures the right people are in the room.
3. **Pricing flexibility.** The $2,800 individual price and $12,000 team price are starting points. Institutions with larger teams, multiple locations, or specific customization needs receive custom quotes.
4. **Post-workshop continuity.** The quarterly check-in schedule (deliverable 5) is agreed upon before the workshop begins, not as an afterthought.

## 3. Session I — The Strategic Landscape (90 minutes)

### 3.1 Purpose

Participants leave this session with a clear, data-driven understanding of where AI is heading in community banking, where their institution stands relative to peers, and a completed AI Maturity Scorecard that quantifies their current position.

### 3.2 Content Specification

**Opening: The efficiency ratio opportunity**

Community banks operate at a median efficiency ratio of approximately 65%, compared to an industry-wide average of approximately 55.7% (FDIC Quarterly Banking Profile, Q4 2024). That gap — roughly 10 percentage points — represents the operational cost of being a community bank. AI does not close the entire gap, but targeted AI deployment in high-frequency, high-time, high-standardization workflows can meaningfully improve the ratio over a 3-year horizon.

**What peer institutions are doing**

- 66% of banks are discussing AI budget allocation (Bank Director 2024 Technology Survey, via Jack Henry)
- 57% of financial institutions report struggling with AI skill gaps (Gartner Peer Community, via Jack Henry)
- 55% have no AI governance framework yet (Gartner, via Jack Henry)
- 48% lack clarity on AI business impacts (Gartner, via Jack Henry)

The implication: the majority of community banks are aware of AI, budgeting for it, but lack the skills, governance, and clarity to deploy it effectively. This is an opportunity window, not a crisis — but the window is closing as larger institutions and fintechs move faster.

**The consumer expectation shift**

- 84% of consumers would switch financial institutions for AI-driven financial insights (Personetics 2025, via Apiture)
- 62% are open to AI-powered fee alerts (2025 consumer survey, via Apiture)
- 76% would switch financial institutions for a better digital experience (Motley Fool, via Apiture)

These numbers are not about AI for AI's sake. They represent member and customer expectations that community banks must meet to retain relationships.

**Case studies: community banks that moved first**

Facilitated discussion of real-world AI deployments at community banks and credit unions. Examples drawn from the Cornerstone Advisors AI Playbook for Banks and Credit Unions (2025), covering:

- Back-office automation: meeting summarization, exception report processing, document routing
- Lending workflow: loan file completeness checks, covenant monitoring, pipeline reporting
- Compliance: regulatory change monitoring, SAR narrative assistance, policy gap analysis
- Member/customer experience: communication personalization, FAQ automation, onboarding

**AI use case landscape by department**

Overview of AI tools and use cases by department, sourced from Cornerstone Advisors:
- Operations: Fathom, Zoom AI Companion, Power Automate, UiPath
- Lending: Document analysis (Claude, ChatGPT), pipeline automation
- Compliance: Regulatory research (Perplexity), policy libraries (NotebookLM)
- Finance: Variance analysis, board reporting, ALCO support
- Retail: Member communications, FAQ automation, service optimization

### 3.3 Activity: AI Maturity Scorecard

**Facilitated exercise (30 minutes within the 90-minute session)**

Participants complete an institutional AI Maturity Scorecard covering six dimensions:

| Dimension | What It Measures | Scoring |
|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| Strategic Clarity | Does leadership have a stated AI vision and prioritized use cases? | 1-5 |
| Governance Readiness | Does the institution have AI policies, a use case inventory, and examiner-ready documentation? | 1-5 |
| Talent and Skills | Has staff been trained? Are there internal AI champions? | 1-5 |
| Technology Foundation | Is the technology stack AI-ready? Are integrations possible? | 1-5 |
| Data Readiness | Is data classified, accessible, and governed? | 1-5 |
| Culture and Change Management | Is leadership supportive? Is there appetite for experimentation? | 1-5 |

**Scoring interpretation:**

| Total Score | Maturity Level | Implication |
|-------------|----------------|-------------|
| 25-30 | Advanced | Ready to scale AI across the institution. Focus on governance and measurement. |
| 19-24 | Developing | Foundation in place. Prioritize governance framework and pilot programs. |
| 13-18 | Early | Awareness exists but infrastructure and skills are lacking. Start with AiBI-Foundation for staff. |
| 6-12 | Starting | Minimal AI engagement. Begin with executive education and strategic visioning. |

**Deliverable:** Completed AI Maturity Scorecard with dimension-level scores and total. This scorecard is referenced in Sessions 2, 3, and 4.

## 4. Session II — Governance and Examiner Readiness (90 minutes)

### 4.1 Purpose

Participants leave this session with a working AI governance framework draft, an understanding of what examiners will ask about AI, and a started AI use case inventory for their institution.

### 4.2 Content Specification

**The regulatory landscape for AI in banking**

There is no comprehensive AI-specific banking regulatory framework yet (GAO-25-107197, US GAO, May 2025). Instead, existing frameworks apply:

- **SR 11-7 (Model Risk Management):** Applies to AI models used in decision-making. Requires model validation, ongoing monitoring, and documentation.
- **Interagency Third-Party Risk Management Guidance:** Applies to AI vendors. Requires due diligence, ongoing monitoring, and concentration risk assessment.
- **ECOA / Reg B:** Applies to any AI used in credit decisions. Requires fair lending analysis and adverse action notice capability.
- **AIEOG AI Lexicon (US Treasury / FBIIC / FSSCC, Feb 2026):** Official definitions for AI governance terms including hallucination, AI governance, AI use case inventory, human-in-the-loop, third-party AI risk, and explainability.

**Examiner expectations: what they will ask**

Examiners are not AI experts. But they are increasingly asking about AI governance. The questions they ask fall into predictable categories:

| Category | Likely Questions | What You Need to Show |
|----------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Inventory | "What AI tools are in use at the institution?" | Complete AI use case inventory with owner, data tier, and risk classification |
| Governance | "Who oversees AI deployment? What policies exist?" | AI governance policy, committee charter or designated owner, approval workflow |
| Risk | "How do you assess AI risk?" | Risk assessment methodology for AI use cases, vendor due diligence documentation |
| Data | "How is data classified and protected?" | Data classification policy covering AI inputs and outputs |
| Third-party | "What AI vendors do you use? How do you evaluate concentration risk?" | Vendor evaluation matrix, concentration risk assessment |
| Compliance | "How do you ensure AI-assisted decisions comply with fair lending and consumer protection requirements?" | Fair lending testing protocol for AI-assisted decisions, adverse action documentation |

**AI use case inventory: building yours in real-time**

Facilitated exercise: participants identify and document AI tools currently in use at their institution (or planned for deployment). For each tool:

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Tool name | What AI tool or platform is used? |
| Department | Which department uses it? |
| Use case | What is it used for? |
| Data tier | What data does it access? (Tier 1: public, Tier 2: internal, Tier 3: restricted) |
| Risk classification | Low / Medium / High based on data sensitivity and decision impact |
| Owner | Who is accountable for this use case? |
| Status | Active / Pilot / Planned |
| Governance status | Governed (policy in place) / Ungoverned (no formal policy) |

**Vendor evaluation and concentration risk**

When a single AI vendor provides critical services across multiple departments, concentration risk increases. The vendor evaluation framework covers:

- **Capability:** Does the vendor solve the stated problem effectively?
- **Security:** Does the vendor meet the institution's data handling requirements?
- **Regulatory alignment:** Can the vendor support examination documentation?
- **Financial viability:** Is the vendor financially stable?
- **Concentration:** What percentage of the institution's AI capabilities depend on this vendor? What is the exit plan?

**Data classification at the board level**

Board members need to understand data classification not at the technical level but at the fiduciary level:

- **Tier 1 (Public):** No AI-related data risk. Freely usable.
- **Tier 2 (Internal):** Requires enterprise-grade AI platforms with acceptable terms of service. No consumer-grade accounts.
- **Tier 3 (Restricted):** PII, account data, examination findings. Never in external AI platforms. On-premise or private cloud only.

The board's role is not to manage data classification — it is to ensure that a data classification policy exists, is enforced, and is reviewed annually.

### 4.3 Activity: AI Governance Framework Draft

**Facilitated exercise (30 minutes within the 90-minute session)**

Participants draft the outline of an AI governance framework for their institution:

1. **Governance owner:** Who is accountable for AI governance? (Individual or committee)
2. **Policy scope:** What does the AI governance policy cover? (Internal use, vendor AI, customer-facing AI)
3. **Approval workflow:** How are new AI use cases approved? (Who proposes, who reviews, who approves)
4. **Risk classification criteria:** How are AI use cases classified by risk level?
5. **Monitoring cadence:** How often are active AI use cases reviewed?
6. **Board reporting:** How is AI activity reported to the board? (Frequency, format, metrics)
7. **Incident response:** What happens when an AI tool produces an error with business impact?

**Deliverable:** AI Governance Framework Draft — a structured outline (not a full policy document) that the institution's compliance team can develop into a formal policy after the workshop.

## 5. Session III — Efficiency Modeling with Your Numbers (90 minutes)

### 5.1 Purpose

Participants leave this session with a 3-year AI roadmap that includes department-by-department ROI projections using their institution's actual financial data. This is the business case that the board can approve.

### 5.2 Content Specification

**Live efficiency ratio modeling**

Using the institution's actual efficiency ratio (collected during the pre-workshop planning call), the facilitator walks through:

1. **Current state:** Where is the institution's efficiency ratio today? How does it compare to the community bank median of approximately 65% (FDIC CEIC data, 1992-2025) and the industry-wide average of approximately 55.7% (FDIC Quarterly Banking Profile, Q4 2024)?
2. **Opportunity sizing:** If targeted AI deployment reduces specific operational costs, what is the efficiency ratio impact? Model scenarios: 2%, 5%, and 8% reduction in non-interest expense.
3. **Realistic targets:** What efficiency ratio improvement is achievable over 3 years given the institution's maturity level (from Session 1 scorecard)?

**Department-by-department AI opportunity sizing**

For each major department, estimate the number of FTEs, the average hours per week spent on automatable tasks, and the projected time savings from AI deployment:

| Department | FTEs | Automatable Hours/Week/FTE | Projected Savings (Year 1) | Projected Savings (Year 3) |
|------------|------|---------------------------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| Operations | — | — | — | — |
| Lending | — | — | — | — |
| Compliance | — | — | — | — |
| Finance | — | — | — | — |
| Retail | — | — | — | — |

The facilitator populates this table with the institution's actual FTE data. Automatable hours estimates are calibrated to the institution's maturity level and technology stack.

**ROI projection methodology**

```
Hourly Rate = Annual Cost per FTE / 2,080 hours
Annual Savings per Department = FTEs x Automatable Hours/Week x Hourly Rate x 50 weeks
Total Annual Savings = Sum across departments
3-Year Cumulative Savings = Year 1 + Year 2 + Year 3 (with adoption ramp: 40% Y1, 70% Y2, 100% Y3)
ROI = Total Savings / Total Investment (training + tools + consulting)
```

**Building the business case**

The business case includes:

1. **Investment required:** AiBI-Foundation for staff ($295/seat), AiBI-S for managers ($1,495/seat), AI tools and platform licenses, consulting engagement if applicable
2. **Projected returns:** Department-by-department savings, efficiency ratio improvement, capacity gained (hours reclaimed, not headcount reduction)
3. **Risk factors:** Implementation risk, adoption risk, regulatory risk, vendor risk
4. **Timeline:** 3-year phased approach aligned with maturity level
5. **Success metrics:** Efficiency ratio, time savings by department, AI use case count, governance compliance

### 5.3 Activity: 3-Year AI Roadmap

**Facilitated exercise (40 minutes within the 90-minute session)**

Participants build a 3-year AI roadmap with three phases:

| Phase | Timeline | Focus | Key Milestones |
|-------|----------|-------|----------------|
| Foundation | Months 1-6 | Staff training (AiBI-Foundation), governance framework, pilot use cases | All staff AiBI-Foundation certified, AI governance policy approved, 3-5 pilot use cases deployed |
| Build | Months 7-18 | Department manager training (AiBI-S), departmental automation, measurement | Key managers AiBI-S certified, 10+ governed use cases, measurable time savings documented |
| Scale | Months 19-36 | Enterprise-wide AI adoption, advanced use cases, continuous improvement | AI embedded in daily operations, efficiency ratio improvement demonstrated, examiner-ready documentation complete |

**Deliverable:** 3-Year AI Roadmap with ROI Projections — a structured document with timeline, investment, projected returns, and success metrics using the institution's actual numbers.

## 8. Facilitation Notes

### 8.1 Facilitation Style

AiBI-L facilitation is executive briefing, not classroom instruction:

- **Pace:** Brisk. No repeating concepts. If a participant asks for clarification, provide it once and move on.
- **Materials:** Printed workbooks (not slides projected on screen). Participants write in the workbook during exercises.
- **Technology:** Minimal. The facilitator uses a laptop for efficiency modeling in Session 3. Participants do not need laptops.
- **Interaction:** Socratic. The facilitator asks questions, not the other way around.
- **Breaks:** Two 15-minute breaks (after S1 and after S3). Working lunch between S2 and S3.

### 8.2 Room Setup

- **Boardroom style** for teams of 8 or fewer (single table, facilitator at head)
- **U-shape** for groups larger than 8
- No classroom-style rows. No podium. No breakout rooms.

### 8.3 Pre-Workshop Facilitator Preparation

The facilitator completes the following before the workshop day:

1. Review all pre-workshop data (financial, organizational, regulatory context)
2. Pre-populate the efficiency model in Session 3 with the institution's actual numbers
3. Research the institution's most recent examination report (if available) for Session 2 context
4. Prepare 3-5 institution-specific case study comparisons (peer institutions of similar size and charter type)
5. Review the participant list and research each participant's role and background
