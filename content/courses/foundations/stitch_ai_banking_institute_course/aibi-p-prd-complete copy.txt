What exists instead is a set of existing frameworks being applied to AI use cases by examiners and regulators. The AIEOG AI Lexicon, published February 2026 by the US Treasury in partnership with FBIIC and FSSCC, represents the most current shared vocabulary. The curriculum uses this vocabulary throughout.FrameworkWhat It IsHow It Applies to AIStaff-Level ImpactSR 11-7Federal Reserve model risk management guidance (2011). OCC and FDIC have confirmed it applies to AI/ML models.Governs traditional ML models like credit scoring, fraud detection, overdraft eligibility. Requires model documentation, validation, and ongoing monitoring.If you use AI in a credit decision or fraud analysis, someone at your institution needs to be able to explain how the model works and document its risk.Interagency TPRM Guidance (2023)OCC/FDIC/Federal Reserve guidance on managing third-party relationships.AI vendors are third-party relationships. Concentration risk, exit provisions, SLA requirements, and due diligence requirements apply.Before your institution signs a contract with an AI vendor, someone needs to run it through the TPRM framework. This is not your job Ń but knowing it exists makes you a better internal voice.ECOA / Reg BEqual Credit Opportunity Act and implementing regulation.Requires institutions to provide adverse action notices for credit decisions. AI-assisted credit decisions must be explainable.If AI helps make a credit decision, the institution must be able to explain why. ŇThe model said soÓ is not a sufficient adverse action notice.BSA / AMLBank Secrecy Act and anti-money laundering requirements.AI is being used in transaction monitoring and SAR narrative drafting. Human oversight requirements remain unchanged.AI can help draft a SAR narrative. The BSA officer still decides what the narrative says and is legally accountable for what is filed.AIEOG AI Lexicon (Feb 2026)Shared AI vocabulary developed by US Treasury, FBIIC, and FSSCC.Establishes common definitions used by regulators and examiners. Not a regulation Ń but the vocabulary your examiner will use.If an examiner asks about your ŇAI use case inventory,Ó this is the document that defines what that phrase means. We use its definitions throughout this course.The AI use case inventory Ń why every staff member needs to know this termAIEOG definition: ŇA maintained repository or listing of an organizationŐs AI use cases, intended to support governance, transparency, and risk management by documenting where and how AI is designed, developed, procured, or used.ÓIn plain language: it is a list of every way your institution uses AI, who owns each use case, what data it touches, and what the output is.
