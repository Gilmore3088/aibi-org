## WHAT THIS DOCUMENT IS

This is the comprehensive PRD for AiBI-S — the specialist course of the AiBI curriculum. It specifies every week, every activity, every deliverable, every platform behavior, and every assessment requirement.

AiBI-S is the second tier in the AiBI certification ladder. It requires AiBI-P as a prerequisite. Where AiBI-P operates at the personal margin of error — the learner builds skills for themselves and can fail privately — AiBI-S operates at the team and institutional margin of error. Everything the learner builds in this course is deployed to their department, used by colleagues, and measured with real data.

The organizing principle of AiBI-S is the progression from personal productivity to institutional capability. The learner arrives with a personal skill (from AiBI-P) and leaves with a documented, governed, measured departmental automation that other people use.

### The Margin of Error Shift

This is the core conceptual upgrade from AiBI-P to AiBI-S:

| Level | Margin of Error | Who is Affected | Course |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|--------|
| Personal | High — fail privately, learn fast, iterate freely | Only you | AiBI-P |
| Team | Medium — colleagues catch mistakes, reputation has some risk but is recoverable | Your immediate colleagues | **AiBI-S (this course)** |
| Institution | Low — documented, governed, examined. Errors have compliance and operational consequences | The whole organization + examiners | **AiBI-S (this course)** |
| Customer-facing | Zero — touches members, borrowers, or depositors. Full governance, full accountability | Members and the public | AiBI-L |

AiBI-P planted the concept. AiBI-S deploys it. The learner moves from "I built something useful for myself" to "I built something my department relies on, and I can prove it works."

### 1.4 Cohort Structure

AiBI-S is delivered as a live cohort, not self-paced. This is a deliberate design decision based on three factors:

1. **Peer accountability.** Department managers deploying AI to their teams need the pressure of weekly deadlines and peer review. Self-paced courses have significantly lower completion rates for working professionals.
2. **Cross-pollination.** The cohort includes learners from all five role tracks. The Week 6 cross-department sharing session is one of the highest-value components — a compliance officer hearing what an operations manager automated (and vice versa) creates institutional connections that outlast the course.
3. **Instructor calibration.** The capstone assessment requires real measured data. An instructor who knows the learner's institution context, role, and constraints can provide feedback that a self-paced review cannot.

**Cohort size:** 12-25 learners per cohort. Minimum 12 ensures at least 2-3 learners per role track for meaningful peer review. Maximum 25 ensures the instructor can provide substantive weekly feedback.

**Schedule:** Weekly 90-minute live sessions, same day and time for the full 6 weeks. Sessions are recorded for learners who miss one session (maximum one missed session without makeup arrangement with instructor).

## 4. Phase I — Foundation (Weeks 1-2)

Two weeks. The learner transitions from personal AI user to institutional AI deployer. They audit their department's workflows and master the platforms relevant to their role track.

### W1 — From Personal Skills to Institutional Assets

**Live Session: 90 minutes**

**Learning goal:** The learner can distinguish between a personal skill and an institutional asset, understands the governance requirements for deploying AI beyond personal use, and has completed a departmental work audit identifying the 10 highest-value automation candidates.

#### Why This Week Exists

AiBI-P graduates have a skill that works for them. Most of them are already using it daily. But that skill lives in their personal account, has no documentation beyond what they wrote for themselves, has no version control, and would be lost if they left the institution. Week 1 closes that gap. The personal skill becomes a template for institutional deployment.

#### Content Specification

**Opening frame: The shift from personal to institutional**

In AiBI-P, the learner built a skill and automated a personal task. That skill is a personal productivity tool — it makes one person faster. In AiBI-S, the question changes: how do you make your department faster? The answer is not "give everyone my skill." The answer involves governance, documentation, versioning, ownership, and measurement. This week introduces each of those concepts.

**Skill governance: what changes when a skill serves a team**

| Dimension | Personal Skill (AiBI-P) | Institutional Skill (AiBI-S) |
|-----------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| Ownership | You own it. You iterate on it when you want to. | Someone owns it. Changes go through a review. |
| Versioning | You save over the old file. Maybe you have a v2. | Version-controlled. Every change documented. Previous versions recoverable. |
| Documentation | You know how it works because you built it. | Someone who did not build it can read the documentation and use it correctly. |
| Handoff | Not applicable — you are the only user. | If you leave, the skill continues to function. No single point of failure. |
| Data handling | You apply the data classification tiers to your own inputs. | The skill documentation specifies what data tier it handles and what must be redacted. |
| Failure modes | You catch your own errors. | The gotcha section documents failure modes so others can catch them. |

**The team skill library concept**

A skill library is not a shared folder full of .md files. A skill library is a curated, maintained, reviewed collection of skills that a department relies on. The distinction matters:

- **Curated:** Not every skill belongs in the library. A skill earns its place by demonstrating value — measured time savings, consistent output quality, and use by more than one person.
- **Maintained:** Skills degrade. Models change. Processes change. Every skill in the library has an owner who reviews it quarterly.
- **Reviewed:** Before a skill enters the library, someone other than the author tests it. This is not bureaucracy — it is quality assurance.

The skill library concept is introduced in W1 and built in W5.

**Work decomposition: identifying "agent-shaped" tasks**

Not every task in a department is a good automation candidate. The work decomposition framework helps learners identify which tasks are worth automating. A task is "agent-shaped" when it meets three criteria:

1. **Frequency:** It happens regularly — at least weekly, ideally daily.
2. **Time:** It takes meaningful time each occurrence — at least 15 minutes.
3. **Standardization value:** The output needs to be consistent every time — same format, same quality, same completeness.

A task that scores high on all three is a strong automation candidate. A task that scores high on frequency but low on standardization value (e.g., creative writing, strategic analysis) is a poor candidate. A task that scores high on time but low on frequency (e.g., annual audit preparation) may be a candidate but will not generate the weekly time savings that demonstrate AiBI-S value.

**The scoring matrix:**

| Score | Frequency | Time per Occurrence | Standardization Value |
|-------|-----------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| 4 | Daily | 60+ minutes | Output must be identical every time |
| 3 | 2-3x per week | 30-59 minutes | Output follows a standard template |
| 2 | Weekly | 15-29 minutes | Output has a general structure but varies |
| 1 | Monthly or less | Under 15 minutes | Output is highly variable each time |

**Maximum possible score per workflow: 12. Minimum threshold for automation candidacy: 7.**

#### Activity 1.1 — Departmental Work Audit

**ACTIVITY 1.1**

Conduct a work audit of your department. Map a minimum of 10 recurring workflows. For each workflow, document:

1. **Name:** What is the task called? (e.g., "Weekly exception report review," "Loan file completeness check")
2. **Current owner:** Who does this today? Is it one person or distributed?
3. **Frequency:** How often does it happen? (daily / 2-3x week / weekly / monthly)
4. **Time per occurrence:** How long does it take each time? (Estimate in minutes. Be honest — track it for one cycle if you can.)
5. **Standardization value:** How consistent does the output need to be? (1-4 scale per matrix above)
6. **Total score:** Frequency + Time + Standardization Value (out of 12)
7. **Current tools:** What tools are used today? (Core system, Excel, Word, email, manual)
8. **Data sensitivity:** What data tier does this task involve? (Tier 1: public, Tier 2: internal, Tier 3: restricted)
9. **Automation feasibility notes:** Any obvious barriers? (e.g., "Requires access to core system data that is not exportable," "Output requires subjective judgment that cannot be templated")

**Submission format:** Completed work audit table (10+ workflows), sorted by total score descending. Top 3 workflows highlighted as primary automation candidates.

**Peer review:** Each learner's work audit is reviewed by one peer from a different role track in the cohort. The peer reviewer answers: "Based on what I see, would I prioritize the same top 3? Why or why not?" This cross-track perspective is one of the highest-value components of the cohort format.

**Due:** Before the W2 live session.

**The departmental workspace concept**

In AiBI-P, learners set up personal Custom Instructions (ChatGPT) or Projects (Claude). In AiBI-S, the concept scales to the department. A departmental workspace is an AI environment pre-loaded with institutional context so that any team member who uses it gets output calibrated to the institution.

| Component | Personal Setup (AiBI-P) | Departmental Setup (AiBI-S) |
|-----------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| Context | Your role, your institution, your preferences | Department mission, team processes, institutional standards, regulatory context |

| Data classification | Your personal awareness of tiers | Explicit data handling rules embedded in workspace instructions |
| Naming | Whatever you called it | Standard naming convention: [Institution]-[Department]-[Function] |
| Access | You only | Any authorized team member can use it or copy it |

## 5. Phase II — First Build (Weeks 3-4)

Two weeks. The learner builds and deploys a real automation for their department and measures its impact with actual data.

### W3 — Build Your First Departmental Automation

**Live Session: 90 minutes**

**Learning goal:** The learner has built and deployed one automation that serves their entire department — not just themselves — and understands the advanced skill architecture patterns required for institutional-grade skills.

#### Why This Week Exists

This is the hardest week of the course. In AiBI-P, the learner built a skill for personal use. The margin of error was high — if the skill produced imperfect output, only the learner saw it. In W3, the learner deploys something that colleagues will use. The margin of error drops to medium. The skill must be robust enough that someone who did not build it can run it and get consistently acceptable output.

The week also introduces advanced skill patterns that were not covered in AiBI-P: multi-step skills, skill chaining, and context engineering at institutional scale.

#### Content Specification

**Advanced skill architecture**

AiBI-P introduced the RTFC framework (Role, Task, Format, Constraint) and the extended skill anatomy (name, description, instructions, output format, gotcha section, constraints). AiBI-S builds on this foundation with patterns for institutional-grade skills:

**Multi-step skills:**

A personal skill typically does one thing: summarize a meeting, draft a response, analyze a document. A departmental skill often needs to do multiple things in sequence: receive input, classify it, apply the appropriate template, produce formatted output, and flag items that require human review.

| Pattern | When to Use | Banking Example |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Single-step | One input, one output, consistent every time | Meeting summary from transcript |
| Sequential multi-step | Input flows through 2-3 processing stages | Loan file received, completeness check run, gap list produced, follow-up email drafted |
| Branching multi-step | Output format depends on input classification | Exception received, classified by type, routed to appropriate response template |
| Skill chaining | Output of Skill A becomes input to Skill B | Regulatory change identified (Skill A: research) then impact assessment produced (Skill B: analysis) then staff communication drafted (Skill C: communication) |

**The 5 Skill Killers at institutional scale**

AiBI-P introduced the 5 Skill Killers from the AIDB pedagogy. In AiBI-S, each killer is revisited through the lens of departmental deployment:

| # | Killer | Personal Impact (AiBI-P) | Institutional Impact (AiBI-S) |
|---|--------|-------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 1 | Description does not trigger properly | Your skill does not activate when you need it | A colleague runs the wrong skill for the task because the description was ambiguous |
| 2 | Over-defining the process | Your output is rigid when you wanted flexibility | Team members cannot adapt the skill to their specific sub-tasks because every step is hardcoded |
| 3 | Stating the obvious | You waste tokens on instructions the model does not need | At departmental scale, wasted tokens multiply. A skill used 20 times per day by 5 people accumulates cost from unnecessary context |
| 4 | Missing gotcha section | You encounter a failure mode and fix it on the fly | A colleague encounters the same failure mode, does not recognize it, and submits flawed output. The gotcha section is the institutional memory of what goes wrong |
| 5 | Monolithic blob | Your skill file is long but you know where everything is | A colleague trying to maintain or update the skill cannot find the component they need to change. Modular structure is a handoff requirement |

**Context engineering for departmental workflows**

Context engineering is the practice of selecting, structuring, and maintaining the background information that an AI skill needs to produce institutionally accurate output. At the personal level, context is "what I need the AI to know about me." At the departmental level, context is "what the AI needs to know about how this department works."

| Context Layer | What It Contains | Who Maintains It | Update Frequency |
|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Institution | Name, charter, asset size, regulators, strategic priorities | AiBI-S graduate or designated AI champion | Annually or on material change |
| Department | Mission, team structure, key processes, technology stack | Department manager (typically the AiBI-S learner) | Quarterly |
| Workflow | Specific process steps, input/output formats, quality standards | Skill owner | On every skill update |
| Regulatory | Applicable frameworks, current guidance, examination findings | Compliance (for all departments) | As guidance changes |

**Data classification enforcement for departmental workflows**

In AiBI-P, data classification was a personal awareness exercise. In AiBI-S, it becomes an enforcement mechanism built into the skill itself.

| Tier | Definition | Departmental Skill Rule |
|------|-----------|------------------------|
| Tier 1: Public | Information available to anyone | No restrictions. Can be used freely in any AI platform. |
| Tier 2: Internal | Non-public institutional information (processes, procedures, internal metrics) | Can be used in AI platforms with enterprise agreements. Skill documentation must specify "Tier 2 data handling" and include redaction instructions for any output that will leave the department. |
| Tier 3: Restricted | Member PII, account data, examination findings, board minutes | Never entered into any external AI platform. Skills that handle Tier 3 data must include explicit constraints: "Do not include member names, account numbers, SSNs, or any data that identifies a specific individual." If the workflow requires Tier 3 data, the skill processes a redacted version. |

#### Role-Track Automation Examples

These are not assignments — they are examples to illustrate the scope and ambition expected for the W3 build. Each learner selects from their work audit (Activity 1.1) the highest-scoring workflow that is feasible to automate in one week.

**Operations (/Ops):**

- Meeting action tracker: Teams meeting transcript processed, action items extracted with owners and deadlines, follow-up emails drafted, tracked in a shared document
- Operational scorecard narrative: monthly metrics data processed into narrative commentary for board reporting

**Lending (/Lending):**
- Loan file completeness check: uploaded loan documents compared against the institution's checklist, gaps identified, condition letter drafted for missing items
- Pipeline status report: raw pipeline data structured into stage-by-stage summary with month-over-month comparison and conversion rate analysis
- Credit policy check: loan scenario evaluated against institution credit policy, exceptions flagged with required documentation notes

**Compliance (/Compliance):**
- Regulatory change monitor: new regulatory guidance researched, summarized, and assessed for impact on institutional policies. Staff communication drafted.
- BSA/AML case narrative assistance: transaction details processed into structured SAR narrative draft (with mandatory [HUMAN REVIEW REQUIRED] flag — BSA officer reviews and approves every output)
- Policy gap analysis: new guidance compared against current institutional policies, gaps identified, remediation recommendations drafted

**Finance (/Finance):**

- Efficiency ratio analysis: quarterly financial data processed into efficiency ratio trends, peer comparison narrative, and improvement opportunity identification

**Retail (/Retail):**
- Member inquiry response library: common inquiry types classified, response templates generated calibrated to institution voice and product set
- Branch daily briefing: calendar, pending items, and key metrics assembled into a structured morning brief for branch managers
- Service recovery communication: complaint details processed, root cause classified, recovery communication drafted with appropriate tone and resolution offer

#### Activity 3.1 — Build and Deploy One Departmental Automation

**ACTIVITY 3.1**

Select the highest-scoring feasible workflow from your work audit (Activity 1.1). Build a complete skill that automates it at departmental scale.

**Requirements:**

1. **Skill file:** Complete extended skill anatomy — name, description (trigger format), instructions, output format, gotcha section, constraints. Skill file under 500 lines. References in separate files if needed.
2. **Context files:** All relevant context documents assembled (institution context, department context, any templates or reference materials the skill needs).
3. **Data classification:** Explicit documentation of what data tier the skill handles and what redaction or restriction rules apply.
4. **Deployment:** The skill is running. At least one colleague has used it (not just you). "Running" means: the skill is available in a workspace or shared location where team members can access and activate it.
5. **Test run documentation:** At least 3 runs with different inputs documented — input provided, output received, quality assessment (acceptable / needs editing / failed).

**Submission format:** Skill file (.md), context files, data classification statement, deployment evidence (screenshot of colleague using it or confirmation from colleague), and 3 test run documents.

**Critical constraint:** The automation must be in use by the department before W4 begins. W4 measures the results. If the learner has not deployed by the W4 session, they cannot complete the time savings measurement required for assessment.

**Due:** Before the W4 live session.

### W4 — Measure and Evaluate

**Live Session: 90 minutes**

**Learning goal:** The learner has measured the actual time savings from their W3 deployment using real before/after data, can evaluate whether AI output meets the quality threshold for departmental use, and has applied a vendor evaluation framework to their role track's platforms.

#### Why This Week Exists

Unmeasured AI adoption is indistinguishable from AI hype. The entire AiBI value proposition rests on the claim that AI can measurably improve community bank operations. W4 is where that claim is tested with real data from the learner's own department.

This week is also where many learners discover that their first automation needs refinement. The output may be "good enough for me" (personal margin of error) but not "good enough for the team" (institutional margin of error). The evaluation methodology in this week provides the framework for identifying and closing that gap.

#### Content Specification

**Evaluation methodology: the quality threshold**

The fundamental question for any departmental automation: is the output good enough to use without editing?

| Quality Level | Definition | Implication |
|---------------|-----------|-------------|
| Use directly | Output can be forwarded, filed, or acted on without any changes | Automation is working. Measure time saved vs. manual process. |
| Light editing | Output needs minor corrections — formatting, word choice, one or two factual details | Automation is useful but the skill needs iteration. Identify which component to fix. |
| Heavy editing | Output structure is correct but significant content changes needed | Skill is underperforming. Root cause is likely in Context or Task components. |
| Unusable | Output would need to be completely rewritten | Skill has a fundamental design problem. Revisit the automation candidate selection — may not be "agent-shaped." |

**Time tracking: before/after measurement**

The time savings measurement is the single most important data point in the AiBI-S assessment. It must be real, not estimated. The methodology:

**Before measurement (baseline):**
- How was this task done before the automation? Manual process described step by step.
- How long did it take? Measured in minutes for at least 3 occurrences in the 2 weeks before deployment. Average calculated.
- Who did it? One person or distributed across the team?
- What was the typical quality? Consistent or variable?

**After measurement (with automation):**
- How is the task done now? Describe the new process including the skill.
- How long does it take? Measured in minutes for at least 3 occurrences during W3-W4. Average calculated.
- Who does it? Same person, different person, or anyone on the team?
- What is the quality? Consistent or variable? Better, worse, or same as before?

**Time savings calculation:**

```
Time saved per occurrence = Before average - After average
Weekly time saved = Time saved per occurrence x Weekly frequency
Annual time saved (projected) = Weekly time saved x 50 weeks
Annual dollar value = Annual hours saved x (Department avg salary / 2080)
```

**The Litmus Test at department scale**

In AiBI-P, the Litmus Test was personal: "If you find yourself having to iterate and refine the output AFTER the skill runs, then the skill itself needs improvement."

At department scale, the Litmus Test adds a second dimension: "Would a new team member produce the same output?" If the answer is no — if the skill only works properly when the person who built it runs it — then the skill has an implicit knowledge dependency that needs to be made explicit in the documentation.

| Litmus Test | Question | Pass Criteria |
|-------------|----------|---------------|
| Output quality | Does the skill produce output that can be used without editing? | 4 out of 5 runs produce "use directly" or "light editing" output |
| User independence | Can someone who did not build the skill get the same quality output? | A colleague runs the skill 3 times and achieves the same quality level as the builder |
| Consistency | Does the skill produce consistent output across different inputs? | No more than 1 "heavy editing" result in 10 runs with varied inputs |

**Vendor evaluation framework**

AiBI-P surveyed six platforms. AiBI-S asks a sharper question: which platforms should your department standardize on? The vendor evaluation framework provides 5 questions to score any AI platform for departmental adoption:

| # | Question | What It Measures | Scoring |
|---|----------|-----------------|---------|
| 1 | Does the platform have an enterprise agreement option with data protection terms? | Institutional risk | 4: Enterprise agreement active. 3: Available, not yet signed. 2: Terms of service only. 1: No enterprise option. |
| 2 | Does the platform support shared workspaces, custom instructions, or team-level configuration? | Departmental scalability | 4: Full team workspace features. 3: Shared templates or custom GPTs. 2: Personal configuration only. 1: No customization. |
| 3 | Can the platform handle the data tier required by your department's workflows? | Data classification compliance | 4: Enterprise data processing agreement covers Tier 2. 3: Acceptable for Tier 2 with precautions. 2: Tier 1 only. 1: Unclear data handling terms. |
| 4 | How does the platform perform on your department's specific task types? | Functional fit | 4: Consistently produces "use directly" output. 3: Produces "light editing" output. 2: Produces "heavy editing" output. 1: Output is unusable for this task type. |
| 5 | What is the total cost per user per year, and is it justified by the time savings measured? | ROI | 4: Cost is under 10% of measured annual time savings value. 3: Cost is 10-25%. 2: Cost is 25-50%. 1: Cost exceeds 50% of savings. |

**Maximum score: 20. Departmental recommendation threshold: 14.**

#### Activity 4.1 — Document Measured Time Savings

**ACTIVITY 4.1**

Document the measured time savings from your W3 deployment. This must use actual data, not estimates.

**Requirements:**

1. **Before data:** At least 3 timed occurrences of the manual process. Each entry: date, duration in minutes, who performed it, notes on quality.
2. **After data:** At least 3 timed occurrences using the automation. Same fields.
3. **Calculation:** Time saved per occurrence, weekly projection, annual projection, dollar value projection using the formula above.
4. **Quality comparison:** Was output quality the same, better, or worse after automation? Evidence required — at least one before/after output comparison.
5. **Board-presentable summary:** A one-paragraph executive summary of the results that could be included in a board report or management presentation. Written in institutional voice, sourced, and conservative in claims.

**Submission format:** Before/after data tables, calculation worksheet, quality comparison document, and the one-paragraph executive summary.

**This is the most important submission in the course.** The assessment rubric has a hard gate on Measured Impact (Dimension 3) — a score below 3 on this dimension is an automatic fail regardless of total score. Estimates, projections without baseline data, or "we think it saves about 2 hours" are not sufficient. Actual timed measurements are required.

**Due:** Before the W5 live session.

## 6. Phase III — Scale and Orchestrate (Weeks 5-6)

Two weeks. The learner standardizes their work into a departmental skill library, creates training materials for handoff, and submits the capstone process improvement package.

### W5 — Build Your Departmental Skill Library

**Live Session: 90 minutes**

**Learning goal:** The learner has built a 3-skill departmental library with documentation, naming conventions, ownership assignments, and training materials that enable handoff to team members who did not build the skills.

#### Why This Week Exists

A single automation is a proof of concept. A skill library is infrastructure. W5 is where the learner transitions from "I built something that works" to "I built something my department can maintain without me." This is the week that creates the institutional capability that outlasts any individual.

The 5-stage organizational skill process (from the AIDB pedagogy) provides the framework. The learner has already done stages 1-3 (Discovery in W1, Curation in W1-W3, Validation in W3-W4). W5 focuses on stages 4-5: Bundling and Ownership.

#### Content Specification

**The 5-stage organizational skill process**

| Stage | Activity | When in AiBI-S | Key Output |
|-------|----------|---------------|------------|
| 1. Discovery | Run work audits. Map the shadow skill landscape. Identify what people are already doing with AI informally. | W1 (Activity 1.1) | Work audit with 10+ workflows scored |
| 2. Curation | Prioritize by frequency x impact x standardization value. Subject matter experts own skills, not engineers. | W1-W3 (workflow selection and build) | Top automation candidates identified and selected |
| 3. Validation | Test against real scenarios. Compare output to manual process. Test with multiple users. | W3-W4 (deployment and measurement) | Measured time savings, quality comparison, Litmus Test results |
| 4. Bundling | Package into the skill library. Define naming conventions, categories, and access. Version control everything. | **W5 (this week)** | 3-skill library with consistent structure |
| 5. Ownership and Maintenance | Assign a champion per skill. Quarterly review schedule. Usage tracking. Deprecation process when skills become obsolete. | **W5 (this week)** | Ownership matrix, review schedule, maintenance protocol |

**Skill library design**

**Naming convention:**

```
[Department]-[Function]-[Version]
```

Examples:
- `ops-exception-triage-v1.md`
- `lending-file-completeness-v2.md`
- `compliance-reg-change-monitor-v1.md`
- `finance-variance-narrative-v1.md`
- `retail-member-response-v1.md`

Rules:
- Lowercase, hyphens only, no spaces
- Department prefix matches the AiBI-S role track code
- Function describes what the skill does, not what it is (verb-noun, not noun)
- Version number increments on every substantive change. Formatting-only changes do not increment.

**Category structure:**

| Category | Description | Examples |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Analysis | Skills that process data and produce insights | Variance analysis, exception triage, pipeline summary |
| Communication | Skills that draft messages, memos, or reports | Board memos, member responses, staff announcements |
| Research | Skills that gather and synthesize information | Regulatory research, market analysis, competitor review |
| Quality Check | Skills that verify completeness or compliance | Loan file check, policy gap analysis, data validation |
| Reporting | Skills that produce structured periodic outputs | Scorecards, dashboards, trend narratives |

**Ownership matrix:**

Every skill in the library has exactly one owner. The owner is responsible for:
- Reviewing the skill quarterly (or when triggered by a model change, tool change, or results degradation)
- Incorporating feedback from users
- Updating the gotcha section when new failure modes are discovered
- Training new team members on how to use the skill
- Deciding when to deprecate the skill

| Skill | Owner | Backup Owner | Last Review | Next Review | Status |
|-------|-------|-------------|-------------|-------------|--------|
| [name] | [person] | [person] | [date] | [date] | Active / Under Review / Deprecated |

**Change management: how to get your team to actually use what you build**

Building a skill library is engineering. Getting colleagues to use it is change management. The two are different disciplines.

| Barrier | What It Looks Like | Countermeasure |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| Skepticism | "I can do it faster manually" | Show the time savings data from W4. Real numbers defeat theoretical objections. |
| Fear | "What if I break something?" | The gotcha section and constraints are designed to prevent this. Walk through them. |
| Inertia | "I'll try it later" | Make the first use effortless. Set up the workspace on their machine. Run it with them once. |
| Not-invented-here | "I have my own way of doing this" | Invite them to contribute their approach to the library. Ownership creates buy-in. |
| Lack of trust | "How do I know the output is accurate?" | Show the validation data from W3-W4. Explain the Litmus Test results. |

**Training materials creation**

A skill that cannot be taught to someone who did not build it is a personal tool, not an institutional asset. W5 requires the learner to create training materials for each skill in their library.

Training materials for each skill must include:

1. **One-page quick start guide:** What the skill does, when to use it (the trigger), how to activate it, what to expect as output.
2. **Input specification:** Exactly what data or content to provide. Examples of good inputs and bad inputs.
3. **Output interpretation guide:** How to read the output. What the sections mean. What requires human review.
4. **Common issues and fixes:** A plain-language version of the gotcha section, written for someone who has never seen the skill internals.
5. **Who to contact:** The skill owner, for questions or when something goes wrong.

#### Activity 5.1 — Create a 3-Skill Departmental Library

**ACTIVITY 5.1**

Build a departmental skill library containing a minimum of 3 skills. One of these is the automation from W3 (which has been measured and validated). The other two are new builds from your work audit top candidates.

**Requirements:**

1. **3 skill files:** Each following the naming convention and extended skill anatomy. Each under 500 lines. References in separate files.
2. **Library index:** A single document listing all 3 skills with: name, category, description (one sentence), owner, backup owner, date created, version, data tier handled.
3. **Ownership matrix:** Completed ownership matrix for all 3 skills with review schedule.
4. **Training materials:** Quick start guide for each of the 3 skills (one page each).
5. **Deployment evidence:** Evidence that at least the W3 skill is in active use by someone other than the builder (screenshot, colleague confirmation, or usage log).

**Submission format:** 3 skill files, library index document, ownership matrix, 3 quick start guides, deployment evidence.

**Peer review:** Each learner's library index and one quick start guide are reviewed by a peer from a different role track. The peer reviewer answers: "Based on this documentation alone — without seeing the skill file — could I understand what this skill does and how to use it?"

**Due:** Before the W6 live session.

## 7. Assessment Rubric

### 7.1 Assessment Structure

The assessment is a five-dimension rubric applied to the full capstone package. Every dimension scores 1-4. Maximum total: 20. Passing score: 14, with a hard gate on Dimension 3 (Measured Impact).

### 7.2 The Hard Gate

**MEASURED IMPACT IS THE HARD GATE**

A score below 3 on Dimension 3 (Measured Impact) automatically fails the submission regardless of total score. A submission with estimated time savings, no baseline data, or projected numbers without actual measurements fails — even if every other dimension is excellent. Resubmission with feedback is available once.

The rationale: The AI Banking Institute's value proposition is that AI measurably improves community bank operations. A specialist who cannot demonstrate measured improvement has not demonstrated specialist-level capability. The hard gate ensures that every AiBI-S credential holder has proven, with real data, that their work produced real results.

### 7.3 Rubric

| Dimension | 1 — Does Not Meet | 2 — Approaching | 3 — Meets Standard | 4 — Exceeds |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|---------------------|-------------|
| **1. Process Selection** — Did they pick a workflow that matters? | Selected a low-frequency or low-impact workflow. Score of 6 or below on the frequency x time x standardization matrix. | Reasonable selection but not the highest-value candidate from the work audit. Score of 7-8. | Selected a high-value workflow. Score of 9-10. Clear justification for selection tied to department priorities. | Selected the highest-impact workflow available. Score of 11-12. Articulated strategic alignment with institutional goals beyond department efficiency. |
| **2. Skill Quality** — Are the deployed skills institutional-grade? | Skills are missing components, contain contradictions, or would not produce consistent output for a user other than the builder. | Skills have most components but gotcha sections are thin, constraints are generic, or documentation assumes builder knowledge. | Skills have all extended anatomy components. Gotcha sections reflect real failure modes observed during deployment. Constraints are specific and enforceable. A colleague could maintain the skill. | Skills are exemplary: modular, well-documented, version-controlled. Gotcha sections are comprehensive. Naming conventions and categorization demonstrate library-scale thinking. Could be handed to another department as a template. |
| **3. Measured Impact** — Real time savings data, not estimates. **HARD GATE: minimum 3 required.** | No baseline data. Savings are estimated or projected without measurement. Before/after data is missing or fabricated. | Some measurement attempted but methodology is inconsistent. Fewer than 3 data points on either side. Quality comparison is subjective without evidence. | At least 3 timed occurrences before and after. Calculation methodology is sound. Quality comparison includes output evidence. Executive summary is conservative and defensible. | Rigorous measurement: 5+ data points on each side. Methodology documented and repeatable. Quality comparison is thorough. Dollar value projection is conservative and sourced. Board-presentable. |
| **4. Documentation** — Can someone who did not build this maintain and iterate on it? | Documentation is incomplete or assumes the reader is the builder. Quick start guides are missing or unusable. | Documentation covers the basics but a new user would have questions. Quick start guides exist but are vague on inputs or output interpretation. | Documentation is complete. A new team member with AiBI-P-level proficiency could use and maintain the skills from documentation alone. Quick start guides are clear and actionable. | Documentation is exemplary. Library index, ownership matrix, and training materials could be adopted by another department without modification. Demonstrates institutional-grade documentation practices. |
| **5. Change Adoption** — Has anyone else on the team actually used it? | No evidence that anyone other than the builder has used the automation. | One colleague has tried it once, but no evidence of ongoing use or feedback incorporation. | At least one colleague has used the automation multiple times. Feedback has been received and incorporated into the skill. Evidence of adoption provided (screenshots, confirmation, usage logs). | Multiple team members are using the automation regularly. Feedback loop is active. The automation has become part of the department's standard workflow. Evidence of organic adoption beyond the builder's direct encouragement. |

### 7.4 Scoring Summary

| Outcome | Criteria |
|---------|----------|
| **Pass** | Total score of 14 or above AND minimum score of 3 on Measured Impact |
| **Fail (hard gate)** | Score below 3 on Measured Impact, regardless of total |
| **Fail (total score)** | Total score below 14, even if Measured Impact is 3+ |
| **Distinction** | Total score of 18 or above with no dimension below 3 |

### 7.5 Resubmission Policy

One resubmission permitted. On initial failure, the learner receives written feedback from the reviewer identifying the specific dimension(s) that fell short and actionable guidance on how to address them. The learner has 14 calendar days to resubmit. For hard gate failures on Measured Impact, the learner must collect additional measurement data — this may require an extension beyond 14 days at the instructor's discretion.

Resubmissions reviewed within 7 business days. The goal is for every learner who puts in the work to eventually earn the credential.

### 7.6 Reviewer Calibration

All capstones reviewed by a certified AiBI reviewer (any AiBI-S or AiBI-L holder with reviewer training). Reviewers calibrated using a shared set of anchor submissions — one per score level per dimension. New reviewers must score three anchor capstones within one point of established scores on every dimension before reviewing live submissions.

Reviewer pool for AiBI-S is initially the founding team at The AI Banking Institute. As AiBI-S and AiBI-L cohorts graduate, the reviewer pool expands. A reviewer must hold AiBI-S or AiBI-L and complete a 2-hour calibration exercise.

## 13. Relationship to Other AiBI Courses

### 13.1 Curriculum Progression

```
Free Assessment → AiBI-P (Practitioner) → AiBI-S (Specialist) → AiBI-L (Leader)
         $0              $295                  $1,495              $2,800+
     12 questions      Self-paced            6-week cohort       1-day workshop
     All staff         All staff             Dept managers        C-suite/Board
     Personal MoE      Personal MoE          Team/Inst MoE       Customer MoE
```

### 13.2 What AiBI-S Feeds Into

AiBI-S graduates are the primary candidates for:

- **AiBI-L workshop:** The graduate understands AI at the operational level and can contribute meaningfully to a leadership-level conversation about governance, roadmaps, and examiner readiness.
- **Consulting engagements (Quick Win Sprint):** The graduate has already identified their department's highest-value automation candidates (from the work audit). The Quick Win Sprint extends this to institution-wide deployment.
- **fCAIO program:** The graduate may become the institution's fractional Chief AI Officer — the internal champion responsible for AI strategy, governance, and skill library maintenance across all departments.

### 13.3 What AiBI-S Does NOT Cover

AiBI-S explicitly does not cover:

- **Customer-facing AI deployment:** That is AiBI-L territory. AiBI-S automations serve the department internally, not members or borrowers.
- **AI governance framework creation:** AiBI-S learners build within an existing governance framework. Creating the framework is an AiBI-L / consulting engagement deliverable.
- **Board-level AI strategy:** AiBI-S produces a board-presentable time savings report, but the strategic roadmap is AiBI-L content.
- **Coding or development:** AiBI-S uses no-code tools (Power Automate, Custom GPTs, Claude Projects, NotebookLM). If a workflow requires custom code, it is escalated to IT or a consulting engagement.
- **MCP architecture or enterprise integrations:** Referenced in the curriculum reference as advanced AIDB content, but deferred from AiBI-S v1. Potential AiBI-S v2 or AiBI-L content depending on community bank technology readiness.
