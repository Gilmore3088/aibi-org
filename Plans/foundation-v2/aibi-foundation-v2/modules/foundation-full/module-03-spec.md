# Module 3: How AI Got Here, in Plain English

**Pillar:** Awareness · **Duration:** 20 min · **Track:** Foundation Full
**Activity types used:** 4 (adaptive scenario timeline), 1 (single-model prompt)
**Daily-use outcomes:** "What this means for my bank" briefing card

---

## Why this module exists

Every conversation with a community-bank CEO eventually includes "okay but explain to me what actually changed in 2022 and why everyone is freaking out now." Bankers are pragmatic; they need historical context to evaluate hype.

This module gives the learner the briefest, most accurate version of "how AI got here," using the AI itself as a teacher — and ends with a one-page briefing card the learner could hand to their CEO or board.

---

## Learning objectives

By the end, the learner will:

1. Explain the 2017→2022→present arc of LLMs in three sentences
2. Distinguish hype from substance when AI is mentioned in news or vendor pitches
3. Articulate why community banks should care now (and why some adjacent claims are exaggerated)
4. Walk away with a briefing card their CEO would actually read

---

## Activities

### Activity 3.1 — Interactive timeline (10 min · type 4)

**Setup (1 min):** "AI didn't appear in 2022. But something *did* change. Walk through the timeline; the platform will surface what mattered and what was noise."

**The activity:**

A scenario-engine timeline with branching exploration. The learner clicks on key moments and gets a 2–3 sentence plain-English explanation. Each click reveals follow-up choices.

Timeline nodes (rotated quarterly as new milestones emerge):

- **2017 — "Attention is All You Need"**: the transformer architecture paper. Plain English: a new way to process language that was much better at long context.
- **2020 — GPT-3**: first time most non-researchers heard about LLMs. Plain English: scale revealed surprising capabilities.
- **November 2022 — ChatGPT public launch**: the consumer moment. Plain English: a polished interface made the underlying technology suddenly accessible to anyone.
- **2023 — Claude, Gemini emerge as competitors**: Plain English: market shifted from "OpenAI" to "AI."
- **2023 — Banking regulators take notice**: Joint Statement on Automated Systems; OCC, FDIC, FRB, CFPB activity. Plain English: existing law applies to AI; no special permission to be careless.
- **2024–2025 — Generative AI in core banking systems**: vendor pitches start citing AI features in LOS, AML, fraud. Plain English: real but uneven; verify substance vs. marketing.
- **2025 — OCC Bulletin 2025-26**: community bank flexibility on model risk management (banks up to $30B). Plain English: regulators acknowledged that small banks can right-size, not skip.
- **2026 — Treasury Financial Services AI RMF**: industry-wide framework, NIST-aligned. Plain English: increasingly the reference examiners use.

At each node, learner picks "tell me more" or "next" or "what does this mean for my bank."

**Capture:** the learner's chosen path through the timeline → a personalized "your AI history briefing" saved as part of the briefing card.

### Activity 3.2 — Hype vs. substance check (5 min · type 6)

**Setup (1 min):** "Not every claim that says 'AI' is meaningful. Three quotes from real (or realistic) industry sources. Tag which is substance, which is hype."

**The activity:**
- Platform displays three quotes (rotated quarterly):
 - One from a real regulator (substantive)
 - One from a vendor pitch (likely hype)
 - One from a peer-bank CEO (real-world signal)
- Learner tags each as: *Substance / Hype / Real-world signal* and writes a one-sentence reason.
- Platform shows author's-eye answer with reasoning.

**Capture:** entries become the "noise vs. signal" section of the briefing card.

### Activity 3.3 — Build your briefing card (5 min · type 1)

**Setup (1 min):** "You're going to draft a one-page briefing your CEO would actually read. The platform will help."

**The activity:**
- Platform pre-fills a one-page briefing card template with the learner's path-through-timeline content and their substance/hype tags.
- Learner uses one prompt to refine: "Make this readable for a community-bank CEO who is busy and pragmatic. Plain English. Honest about what's real and what's hype. Under 350 words."
- Platform sends to a default model (Claude or GPT — author's choice for current quality).
- Learner edits in their voice.

**Capture:** the final briefing card → `module_03_what_this_means_for_my_bank.md`.

## Daily-use outcomes

1. A **one-page CEO briefing** the learner can literally hand to their executive sponsor
2. A **calibrated sense** of which AI claims to take seriously and which to treat as marketing
3. A **historical anchor** that prevents being swept up in hype or paralyzed by skepticism

---

## Assessment criteria

- Timeline path completed (at least 6 of 8 nodes explored)
- Substance/hype tagging shows reasoning, not random guesses
- Final briefing card is under 400 words and reads like a banker would read it

---

## Author/facilitator notes

- **Quarterly refresh is critical here.** Add new nodes as milestones occur. Drop or de-emphasize older ones if they no longer matter.
- **Keep regulator citations factual and current.** This module touches the same regulatory references as Module 7 — keep consistency.
- **The "what does this mean for my bank" branches are the secret sauce.** Don't shortchange them. They're the bridge from history to action.
- **Don't make this module a hype-fest.** Bankers can spot it. Keep the tone honest, slightly dry, banker-to-banker.
